The Consumer Financial Protection Bureau is the primary federal regulator overseeing Equifax, Experian, and TransUnion. Filing a complaint with the CFPB creates an official record that the bureau must respond to — and the CFPB publishes complaint data publicly, which motivates bureaus to resolve issues quickly.

This isn't a last resort — it's a legitimate escalation step after a failed or improper dispute investigation.

## Step 1: Gather Your Documentation

Before filing, assemble: your original dispute letter, the certified mail delivery confirmation, the bureau's investigation results letter, and any other correspondence. The more documented your complaint, the more effective it will be.

## Step 2: Go to consumerfinance.gov/complaint

Select "Credit reporting, credit repair services, or other personal consumer reports" as the product. Then select the sub-issue that best matches your situation — options include "incorrect information on your report," "problem with investigation into an existing problem," or "improper use of your report."

## Step 3: Identify the Company

Select the specific bureau (Equifax, Experian, or TransUnion) you're complaining about. You can also file complaints against collection agencies if the issue involves a furnisher violation.

## Step 4: Describe the Issue Clearly

In the complaint narrative, be specific and factual. Include: the date you sent your dispute, the date the bureau received it (from your certified mail receipt), the date of the bureau's response, what the bureau said, and why the investigation was inadequate. Reference FCRA provisions where applicable.

## Step 5: Attach Supporting Documents

Upload your dispute letter, the return receipt, and the bureau's response. Concrete documentation significantly increases the weight of your complaint.

## What Happens After You File

The CFPB forwards your complaint to the bureau, which has 15 days to respond. You receive the bureau's response through the CFPB portal and can dispute it if unsatisfactory. Complaint outcomes are logged in the CFPB's public Consumer Complaint Database.

## Following Up

If the bureau's response to the CFPB complaint still doesn't resolve the issue, escalate further by consulting a consumer rights attorney. Your CFPB complaint and documentation are exactly what an attorney needs to evaluate your case.
