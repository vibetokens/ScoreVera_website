When your dispute is denied and the bureau says the item was "verified," your next move is to demand they explain exactly how they verified it. A method of verification letter invokes your rights under FCRA § 611(a)(7) and forces the bureau to describe their investigation process. This is not just a follow-up request — it's a legal demand with a specific statutory basis.

## What to Include in a Method of Verification Letter

Your letter should contain the following:

**Your identifying information** — full name, current address, last four of SSN, and date of birth.

**Reference to the original dispute** — the date you sent the dispute, the certified mail tracking number, and the date the bureau responded.

**The specific disputed item** — creditor name, account number, and what you disputed.

**The legal demand** — state clearly that under FCRA § 611(a)(7), you are requesting a description of the procedure used to determine the accuracy of the disputed item, including the name, business address, and telephone number of any person contacted in connection with the investigation.

**The statutory deadline** — note that you are submitting this request within 15 days of receiving the verification notice as required by § 611(a)(7).

## Tone and Format

Keep the letter professional and factual. Do not make demands beyond what the statute authorizes. The letter should be one to two pages. Send via Certified Mail with Return Receipt.

## What You Should Receive Back

The bureau must describe the investigative procedures — in practice, this typically means disclosing that they contacted the furnisher through E-OSCAR and received a verification code. If that's all they did, you now have documentation showing that no original documents were reviewed.

## Using the Response

Attach the bureau's method of verification response to any CFPB complaint you file. It is direct evidence of whether the bureau conducted a reasonable investigation as the FCRA requires.
