## Getting Your Free Credit Reports

Before you can read your credit report, you need to get it. The official source is AnnualCreditReport.com — the only federally mandated free credit report service. You're entitled to free weekly reports from all three major bureaus: Equifax, Experian, and TransUnion. This expanded access (previously once per year) became permanent after the COVID-19 pandemic.

Each bureau maintains a separate report, and they don't share data with each other in real time. Your Equifax report may look different from your TransUnion report. Review all three.

## Section 1: Personal Information

The first section contains identifying information the bureaus use to match data to your file:

- **Name:** May include variations (maiden name, nicknames, Jr./Sr.)
- **Current and previous addresses**
- **Date of birth**
- **Social Security number:** Usually partially masked (e.g., XXX-XX-1234)
- **Phone numbers**
- **Employers:** Current and past, as reported by creditors

What to look for: Any address you've never lived at, any name that isn't yours or a clear variation, any SSN that doesn't match yours. These can signal a mixed file or identity theft.

## Section 2: Account History (Trade Lines)

This is the largest and most important section. Each credit account you've had — credit cards, auto loans, mortgages, personal loans, student loans, and sometimes retail accounts — appears here as a separate "tradeline." For each account, you'll see:

- **Creditor name** and account number (usually partially masked)
- **Account type** (revolving, installment, mortgage)
- **Date opened** and date closed (if applicable)
- **Credit limit or original loan amount**
- **Current balance**
- **Monthly payment**
- **Account status** (Open, Closed, Charged Off, In Collections, etc.)
- **Payment history:** Usually shown as a grid — each month is marked OK (on time), 30, 60, 90, 120+ (days late), or blank/not reported

What to look for: Accounts you don't recognize (potential identity theft), balances that are higher than they should be, late payment marks you believe are wrong, accounts that should be closed but show as open, wrong credit limits.

## Section 3: Collections

Collection accounts appear separately from your original account history. Each collection entry will show:

- The collection agency's name
- The original creditor's name
- The original amount and current balance
- The date the collection was opened
- The date of first delinquency (critical for the seven-year reporting clock)

What to look for: Collections you don't recognize, paid collections that still show a balance, collections with a date of first delinquency that seems too recent (potential re-aging), medical collections under $500 or paid medical collections (which should have been removed under the bureaus' 2023 policies).

## Section 4: Public Records

Bankruptcies are the primary public record that appears on credit reports. (Judgments and tax liens were removed from credit reports by the bureaus several years ago.) A Chapter 7 bankruptcy stays on your report for 10 years from the filing date; a Chapter 13 stays for 7 years.

What to look for: A bankruptcy that isn't yours, an incorrect filing date, accounts that were discharged in bankruptcy but still show an active balance.

## Section 5: Inquiries

The inquiries section lists every entity that accessed your credit report. It's divided into two parts:

- **Hard inquiries:** From applications for credit. Visible to lenders, impact your score, stay on your report for two years.
- **Soft inquiries:** From you checking your own credit, pre-approval screenings, employer background checks. Visible only to you, no score impact.

What to look for: Hard inquiries from companies you've never applied to — these may indicate fraud or an unauthorized credit pull.

## How to Read a Payment History Grid

The payment history grid is often one of the most confusing parts of a credit report. Each column represents a month (reading right to left, with the most recent on the left). The codes mean:

- **OK or "Current":** Paid on time
- **30, 60, 90, 120:** Days past due
- **CO:** Charged off
- **ND or blank:** No data for that month
- **X:** Account was not yet open

## What to Do After Reading Your Report

After reviewing all three reports, make a list of everything that looks wrong. Prioritize: inaccurate negative items (wrong late payments, collections that are errors) should be addressed first, as they have the biggest impact on your score. Use the credit bureau's online dispute portal, their phone line, or a written dispute letter to initiate corrections.

Reading your report once a year — or better, once every four months rotating between the three bureaus — is the most powerful free tool you have for protecting your credit.
