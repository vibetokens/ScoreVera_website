## Negative marks don't last forever

One of the most common misconceptions about credit is that a mistake follows you for life. It doesn't. Federal law — specifically the Fair Credit Reporting Act (FCRA) — sets firm limits on how long negative information can appear on your credit report. Knowing these timelines can help you plan and give you something concrete to look forward to.

## The 7-year rule

Most negative items fall off your credit report after **seven years**. This includes:

- **Late payments** (30, 60, 90, 120+ days late)
- **Collection accounts**
- **Charge-offs** (when a lender writes your debt off as a loss)
- **Repossessions**
- **Foreclosures**
- **Judgments** (in most states)
- **Settled accounts**

Seven years is a long time, but here's the important part: the negative impact fades well before the item actually disappears. A late payment from six years ago has very little effect on your score compared to one from last year. Time heals credit — slowly, but reliably.

## Bankruptcy is different

Bankruptcy stays on your credit report longer:

- **Chapter 7 bankruptcy**: 10 years from the filing date
- **Chapter 13 bankruptcy**: 7 years from the filing date

Chapter 13 gets the shorter timeline because it involves a repayment plan — you paid back at least some of what you owed. Chapter 7 is a full discharge, so it stays longer.

## When does the clock start? The Date of First Delinquency

This is where many people get confused — and where some shady debt collectors try to manipulate things. The seven-year clock starts from the **Date of First Delinquency (DOFD)**: the date you first missed a payment that led to the negative status.

It does NOT restart because:
- A debt was sold to a new collection agency
- You made a partial payment
- The collector called you or sent a letter
- The debt was re-aged on the report (this is illegal)

If a collection account shows a "date opened" or "date reported" that's more recent than the original delinquency, that's a red flag. The DOFD is what determines when it must come off your report — not when it was sold or when a collector first listed it.

## What falls off automatically

Under § 605 of the FCRA, credit bureaus are legally required to remove negative items once their reporting period expires. You don't have to do anything — it's supposed to happen automatically.

In practice, some items linger past their expiration date due to bureau errors. If you notice a negative item that should have aged off, file a dispute with the bureau citing the FCRA's maximum reporting period. Include documentation of the original delinquency date if you have it. Bureaus typically remove expired items quickly once disputed.

## Items that don't have a time limit

A few things stay on your report indefinitely:
- **Positive accounts** — good accounts with on-time payments can stay on your report as long as the account is open, and often for 10 years after it's closed
- **Unpaid tax liens** — federal tax liens can be reported indefinitely if unpaid (though the IRS has relaxed reporting of tax liens in recent years)
- **Criminal convictions** — not on credit reports, but worth noting

## How to use this information

If you have a collection from 2019 or a late payment from 2020, calculate when it falls off and write that date down. As it gets closer to expiration, the damage decreases. You can dispute expired items directly through the bureau's website — it takes 10 minutes and they have 30 days to respond.

Credit problems have expiration dates. Knowing yours is part of your recovery plan.
